The support is all real numbers: $(-\infty, \infty)$.

\easysubproblem{What is the cumulative distribution function of the normal random variable?}
\\The CDF does not have a closed-form algebraic solution and is expressed as an integral:$$F(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{x} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{t-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2} \,dt$$

\easysubproblem{If $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ then what is $\mathbb{E}[X]$?}

It is a special normal random variable with a mean of $0$ and a variance (and standard deviation) of $1$. It is typically denoted by the letter $Z$, where $Z \sim N(0, 1)$.

\easysubproblem{Define what is meant by a joint mass function (jmf). }

A joint probability mass function $p(x, y) = P(X = x, Y = y)$ is a function that gives the probability that two discrete random variables, $X$ and $Y$, simultaneously take on specific values $x$ and $y$. It must satisfy $p(x,y) \ge 0$ and $\sum_x \sum_y p(x,y) = 1$.

\intermediatesubproblem{Find $P(X \leq 2, Y \leq 2)$.}

$$P(X \le 2, Y \le 2) = \sum_{x \le 2} \sum_{y \le 2} p(x, y)$$

\intermediatesubproblem{Find $f_X(2)$.}

This represents the marginal probability that $X = 2$. Find the row where $X=2$ and sum all the values across all columns ($Y$ values) in that row.

\intermediatesubproblem{Find $f_Y(4)$.}

This represents the marginal probability that $Y = 4$. Find the column where $Y=4$ and sum all the values down all rows ($X$ values) in that column.

\intermediatesubproblem{Find $f_X(x)$.}

$f_X(x) = \sum_{all \, y} p(x, y)$ (The row totals)

\intermediatesubproblem{Find $f_Y(y)$.}

$f_Y(y) = \sum_{all \, x} p(x, y)$ (The column totals)

\inthenotessubproblem{What does it mean for two random variables, $X$ and $Y$, to be independent?}

Two discrete random variables are independent if and only if their joint PMF is equal to the product of their marginal PMFs for all possible pairs of $(x, y)$:$$p(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$$

\hardsubproblem{For the joint probability mass function specified above, is $X$ and $Y$ independent? Explain why or why not.}

To check: Pick a single cell in your matrix, say $(x=1, y=1)$. \\Multiply its row total ($f_X(1)$) by its column total ($f_Y(1)$).\\If $f_X(1) \cdot f_Y(1) = p(1,1)$, check the other cells. \\If it holds true for every single cell, they are independent.\\If even a single cell fails this multiplication equality, write: \\"No, because $p(x,y) \neq f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$ for cell $(x,y)$."

\end{enumerate}

\easysubproblem{Explain what is meant by a convolution of random variables.}

A convolution is a mathematical operation used to find the probability distribution of the sum of two or more independent random variables ($T = X + Y$). For discrete variables, it involves summing over all possible mutually exclusive ways the individual variables can add up to a target value $t$.

\intermediatesubproblem{Suppose $X_1, X_2 \iid \geometric{p}$}. Let $T = X_1 + X_2$. What is the support of $T$?

Support: $\{2, 3, 4, 5, \dots\}$

\hardsubproblem{Find $P(T = 2)$.}

The only way to get a sum of 2 is if $X_1 = 1$ and $X_2 = 1$:$$P(T=2) = P(X_1=1) \cdot P(X_2=1) = p \cdot p = p^2$$

\hardsubproblem{Find $P(T = 3)$.}

Ways to sum to 3: $(1,2)$ or $(2,1)$.$$P(T=3) = P(X_1=1, X_2=2) + P(X_1=2, X_2=1) = p(q p) + (q p)p = 2p^2q \quad \text{(where } q = 1-p\text{)}$$

\hardsubproblem{Find $P(T = 4)$.}

Ways to sum to 4: $(1,3)$, $(2,2)$, or $(3,1)$.$$P(T=4) = p(q^2p) + (qp)(qp) + (q^2p)p = 3p^2q^2$$

\hardsubproblem{Based off the last three computations, can you guess the probability mass function of $T$? You do not have to mathematically prove your answer. (If you are curious, the actual proof requires induction and the general convolution formula which you are not required to know).}

Following the pattern ($1p^2$, $2p^2q^1$, $3p^2q^2$):$$P(T = t) = (t - 1)p^2(1-p)^{t-2} \quad \text{for } t = 2, 3, 4, \dots$$

\end{enumerate}

\easysubproblem{Without simply quoting the formal statement of the Central Limit Theorem, can you explain what the Central Limit Theorem states?}

The CLT states that if you take a large sample size ($n \ge 100$) of independent, identically distributed random variables from any underlying population distribution (even skewed or weird ones) with a finite mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the distribution of the sample sum (or sample mean) will naturally form a Normal Distribution shape.

\hardsubproblem{ Suppose the random variables, $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_{100}$ are independent and all have the following density:
\begin{Large}
$$
f(x) =
\begin{cases}
\frac{x^2}{72} & \text{if} ~ 0<x<6 \ \\ 
0 , & \text{otherwise} \\
\end{cases}
$$ 
\end{Large}

If $Y = X_1 + X_2 + \ldots + X_{100}$, then find/approximate $P(439 \leq Y \leq 461)$. You may leave your answer in terms of an integral, in terms of $\Phi$, in terms of a calculator command, or in terms of an actual number rounded to four decimal places.
 }

Step 1: Find the mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ of a single variable $X$.$$\mu = E[X] = \int_{0}^{6} x \cdot \frac{x^2}{72} \,dx = \frac{1}{72} \left[ \frac{x^4}{4} \right]_{0}^{6} = \frac{1296}{288} = 4.5$$$$E[X^2] = \int_{0}^{6} x^2 \cdot \frac{x^2}{72} \,dx = \frac{1}{72} \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} \right]_{0}^{6} = \frac{7776}{360} = 21.6$$$$\sigma^2 = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = 21.6 - (4.5)^2 = 21.6 - 20.25 = 1.35$$Step 2: Find the expected value and variance for the sum $Y$.$$\mu_Y = n\mu = 100(4.5) = 450$$$$\sigma^2_Y = n\sigma^2 = 100(1.35) = 135 \implies \sigma_Y = \sqrt{135}$$By CLT, $Y \approx N(450, 135)$. Standardizing gives:$$P(439 \le Y \le 461) \approx \text{normalcdf}(439, 461, 450, \sqrt{135})$$Answer: $\Phi\left(\frac{11}{\sqrt{135}}\right) - \Phi\left(\frac{-11}{\sqrt{135}}\right) \approx 0.6551$
